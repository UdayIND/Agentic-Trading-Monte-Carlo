# MARKET HISTORY — Episodes and Transferable Lessons

Reference module. History doesn't repeat exactly; the *mechanisms* do:
leverage, liquidity, crowding, narrative, forced selling. Each episode:
what happened, the mechanism, and the standing lesson this system encodes.

| Episode | What happened | Mechanism | Standing lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1929–1932 | −89% Dow peak-to-trough over ~3 years | Margin leverage 10:1; deflationary spiral; policy tightening into weakness | Drawdowns can last years, not weeks; leverage converts decline into ruin |
| Nifty Fifty (1972–74) | "One-decision" quality stocks −60–80% | Quality at any price; multiple compression | Quality does not immunize against valuation (knowledge/investing-styles §3) |
| 1987 Black Monday | −22.6% in ONE day | Portfolio insurance = mechanical selling into decline; liquidity vacuum | Gap risk is real: sizing must survive a −20% overnight gap (trade-plan field #9) |
| Japan 1989→ | Nikkei −80%; ~34 years to reclaim the high | Twin bubbles (equity+land), demographic overhang, zombie banking | "The index always comes back" is a US-sample artifact; time-to-recovery is a risk dimension |
| Dot-com (2000–02) | NASDAQ −78%; profitless narrative stocks −95%+ | Story > cash flow; supply of new paper met exhausted demand | Real businesses with no earnings path are optionality, size them as such; QQQ itself took 15 years to reclaim 2000 highs |
| 2008 GFC | SPX −57%; credit system near-failure | Housing leverage, securitization opacity, funding runs; correlations → 1 | Diversification fails exactly when needed; credit leads equity; cash is a position |
| 2010 Flash Crash | −9% intraday, minutes | HFT liquidity withdrawal; stub quotes | Market orders into stress = fills at absurdity — the no-plain-market-orders rule |
| 2011, 2015, 2018 corrections | −19–20% index swoons, no recession | Positioning + policy scares (debt ceiling, yuan, QT) | Most corrections aren't crises; the regime matrix exists to avoid treating every dip as 2008 |
| Volmageddon (Feb 2018) | Short-vol ETPs −90%+ overnight | Crowded short-vol carry; feedback via VIX futures | Selling tails harvests pennies before a steamroller; Sharpe of carry strategies lies (quant §1) |
| COVID crash (Mar 2020) | −34% in 23 sessions; fastest bear ever | Exogenous shock; liquidity spiral incl. Treasuries; then fastest recovery on policy flood | Speed of modern drawdowns beats discretionary reaction — pre-committed stops/breakers or nothing; missing the recovery is also a risk |
| Meme squeezes (Jan 2021) | GME +2,400% then −90% | Gamma + short squeeze + social coordination | Flow can overwhelm fundamentals for weeks; shorting narrative stocks is uncapped risk; not our game |
| 2022 rate shock | SPX −25%, NASDAQ −33%, 60/40 worst year in decades; bonds fell WITH stocks | Fastest hiking cycle in 40y; duration repricing everywhere | Stock/bond anticorrelation is regime-dependent, not a law; long-duration equity = rates exposure |

## The recurring mechanisms

1. **Leverage** turns being early into being dead. Every catastrophe has it.
2. **Liquidity is a fair-weather friend** — exits price at panic, not at
   model marks. Position sizes must respect exit liquidity.
3. **Crowding**: the trade that "always works" accumulates the population
   that guarantees it eventually won't. Consensus positioning is a risk
   input (intelligence report sentiment section).
4. **Narrative peaks coincide with supply**: issuance floods (IPO/SPAC
   waves, secondaries) mark late cycle better than valuation does.
5. **Policy is the pivot**: most major bottoms are policy events, most
   major tops follow tightening. Fighting the policy direction is a
   low-expectancy stance.
6. **Recovery asymmetry**: −50% needs +100%; recoveries also cluster —
   the best days sit inside the worst months, which is why the breaker
   *re-arms* (Art IV amendment) rather than exiting markets indefinitely.

## Base rates worth memorizing (US large-cap, ~1928–present, approximate)

- Intra-year drawdowns average ~14% even in positive years.
- −10% corrections: roughly every 1–2 years. −20% bears: every ~4–6 years.
- Bear markets average ~−33% and ~9–18 months; recoveries usually longer.
- Missing the 10 best days per decade roughly halves compounded returns —
  and those days cluster near the worst ones (both facts argue for rules,
  not forecasts, governing exposure).
- Since 1929 the S&P has NEVER failed to eventually reclaim highs — in the
  US sample. Japan is the reminder that this is a sample, not a theorem.
